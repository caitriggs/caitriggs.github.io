A Book Report · No. 1

The E-Myth Revisited

Why most small businesses don't work — and what to do about it.

By Michael E. Gerber Notes by Cait
For Max

I read this and kept thinking about us. Not in a fix-it way — in a "this is the language for the thing we keep circling" way. Tinker with it. Argue with it. Then tell me what you think.

§ 01 — The Big Idea

Most businesses aren't started by entrepreneurs.

They're started by skilled people having a really bad day at work.

Gerber calls this moment the "Entrepreneurial Seizure" — the day a technician decides she can do this better on her own. The problem isn't ambition. The problem is what Gerber calls the Fatal Assumption: that knowing how to do the work means knowing how to run the business that does the work.

The technical work of a business, and a business that does that technical work, are two different things.
— Paraphrased from Gerber, Ch. 2

Most small businesses fail because their founders never learn the difference.

§ 02 — The Cast

Every owner contains three people.

The Entrepreneur. The Manager. The Technician. They all want to be the boss. None of them wants to have a boss. So they start a business together — and the trouble begins.

The Entrepreneur
The Visionary

Lives in the future. Sees what could be. Builds the house, and the moment it's done starts designing the next one. Creates change — and a lot of mess.

"The Entrepreneur lives in the future, never in the past, rarely in the present."
The Manager
The Pragmatist

Lives in the past. Craves order. Sees problems where the Entrepreneur sees opportunities. Builds neat rows of things. Cleans up after the visionary.

"The Manager craves order, compulsively clings to the status quo."
The Technician
The Doer

Lives in the present. Loves the feel of the work itself. Suspicious of abstraction. Believes thinking gets in the way of doing. "If you want it done right, do it yourself."

"Thinking isn't work; it gets in the way of work."

Gerber's argument isn't that any one of these is wrong. It's that most owners aren't balanced — and one personality runs the whole show.

§ 04 — The Distinction

Working in the business vs. working on it.

This is the line that ran through every chapter for me. The Technician's question is "what work has to be done today?" The Entrepreneur's question is "how must the business work?" Both matter. But only one of them builds something that outlasts the founder.

"If your business depends on you, you don't have a business — you have a job."
— Gerber's central provocation

The fix isn't to stop doing the technical work. It's to stop letting the technical work consume all the other roles the business needs.

§ 05 — The Prototype

Build it as if you'll franchise it 5,000 times.

Even if you never sell or franchise — design the business as a prototype. A system so well documented that an ordinary, well-trained person could run it without you in the room.

The prototype is how the entrepreneur's vision gets turned into something real and repeatable.

This is the part that I think reframes everything. The work isn't doing the thing. The work is designing the system that does the thing. The technical skill doesn't go away — it gets encoded, repeatable, transferable.

§ 06 — The Receipts

Five takeaways worth keeping.

  1. The E-Myth itself. Most businesses are founded by technicians, not entrepreneurs. Technical skill is necessary and insufficient.
  2. Work on it, not in it. If the business can't function without you, it's a job dressed up in business clothes.
  3. Three personalities, one owner. Entrepreneur, Manager, Technician. The dominance of any single one is the imbalance.
  4. Systems run the business; people run the systems. Design for ordinary operators executing extraordinary systems — not heroes burning out.
  5. Start with the end in mind. Mature companies began with the picture of the mature company. Then worked backward.
§ 07 — A Note
— PERSONAL —

The reason I wanted you to see this isn't because I think you're "too much Technician." It's because the book gave me language for something I've been circling — that the work of building Rad Rigs (and what comes next) is a different kind of work than the work of restoring a rig perfectly.

Both matter. Gerber just makes the case that the second one has to make room for the first.
