Shared Easement Project — West Seattle

Grub Treatment & Replanting Schedule

Spring 2026 → Spring 2027 Prepared April 2026 Shared with neighbors
Background: We lost mature birch trees (as tall as our townhomes) to wood-boring beetle damage over 1–2 years, dying top-down one at a time. Following a $15,000 easement replanting, we subsequently lost a Japanese Stewartia and all of our blue fescue grass. The exact cause of those losses is not fully confirmed, but likely involves some combination of: soil-dwelling beetle grubs eating plant roots (European chafer and/or other scarab species common in West Seattle), inadequate watering during the Stewartia’s establishment period, and/or poor drainage at the planting site. Grub species have not been formally identified — Phase 1 of this schedule confirms what we’re dealing with before we treat.

Key principle: Diagnose first, then treat the soil before spending another dollar on plants. The Swanson Nursery rep confirmed this sequencing and specifically recommended testing drainage at the Stewartia site. ↗ WSN: White Grubs & Beetles in Our Lawns (Feb 2025) ↗ WSN: European Chafer Grubs (Mar 2025) ↗ Swanson Nursery in-person consultation, March 2026

Step 1 — Diagnose
Confirm grubs & test soil drainage
+
  • 01
    Grub check: At the edge of any dead grass patch, cut three sides of a 12"×12" square of turf like a flap. Peel it back and count C-shaped white larvae with brown heads in the top 2–3 inches of soil. 10 or more per square foot confirms active infestation. ↗ WSN: European Chafer Grubs↗ PNW Pest Management Handbook: White Grub
  • 02
    Drainage (percolation) test at the exact spot where the Stewartia died. Dig a hole 12" wide × 12" deep. Fill with water and let drain overnight. Refill the next morning. Measure water level drop every hour for 3 hours. Target: 1–3 inches per hour. Under 1 inch = drainage problem that needs fixing before replanting. ↗ Swanson Nursery in-person recommendation, March 2026↗ Today's Homeowner: DIY Soil Drainage Perk Test
  • 03
    Report findings to neighbors and decide whether drainage amendment (compost/raised soil) is needed before we replant in late May. Share results of both tests before proceeding.
Cost this phase: $0 — DIY diagnostics only. No product purchases yet.
April–May 2026
Step 2 — Treat
First grub treatment: kill spring larvae before pupation
+
  • 01
    Purchase GrubGONE! (Bacillus thuringiensis galleriae / Btg). This is the West Seattle Nursery-recommended bio-insecticide effective against all grub life stages. Safe for pollinators, pets, and beneficial insects.

    GrubGONE! by Green Earth Ag & Turf Buy at: West Seattle Nursery
  • 02
    Water the area thoroughly the day before application so soil is moist but not waterlogged. Dry soil prevents penetration.
  • 03
    Mix and apply per label. Dissolve in water in a hose-end sprayer or watering can. Apply evenly over all affected areas, bare patches, and anywhere turf feels loose or spongy. Cover the full easement area.
  • 04
    Water in immediately after application to drive the treatment 2–3 inches into the soil where grubs are actively feeding.
  • 05
    Repeat every 2–4 weeks through May before grubs pupate into adult beetles. This is the last window to reduce this year's generation before they emerge and lay new eggs.
Why this timing matters: Grubs are rising from overwintering now and actively feeding at the root zone. After they pupate in late May, soil treatment has little effect until the next egg hatch cycle in July. ↗ WSN: White Grubs & Beetles in Our Lawns (Feb 2025)↗ WSN: European Chafer Grubs (Mar 2025)
May 2026
Step 3 — Treat
Adult beetle deterrence: foliar neem spray
+
  • 01
    Purchase neem oil concentrate (look for “azadirachtin” or “clarified hydrophobic extract of neem oil” on the label).

    Bonide Neem Oil Concentrate or similar azadirachtin product Buy at: WSN, hardware store, or Amazon

    NOT a soil treatment — foliar only
  • 02
    Mix: 2 tablespoons neem oil concentrate per 1 gallon water, plus a few drops of dish soap as emulsifier. Use a pump sprayer.
  • 03
    Spray in the evening only — never in direct sun or when bees are active. Coat foliage of nearby shrubs, plants, and ground cover. This deters adult beetles from feeding and laying eggs on plant surfaces.
  • 04
    Repeat every 7–14 days through July during adult flight season.
  • 05
    Do not mow below 3 inches. Closely cropped turf is more attractive to egg-laying female beetles. ↗ WSN: White Grubs & Beetles in Our Lawns (Feb 2025)
Late May 2026
Step 4 — Prep
Soil amendment & pre-planting prep
+
  • 01
    If drainage test from April failed (under 1 inch/hour): amend soil at the Stewartia planting site with compost mixed to 12" depth, or build up soil level slightly to improve drainage before planting.
  • 02
    Clear dead plant material. Remove all dead blue fescue and any dead ground cover. Do not compost grub-damaged material — bag and dispose of it.
  • 03
    Apply first round of mulch (4–5 bags) across the easement area after clearing dead plant material and before planting. Spread 2–3 inches deep over all bare soil. Keep 6 inches away from any existing plant stems and the Stewartia trunk once planted. This suppresses weeds, retains moisture, and moderates soil temperature during the treatment and planting period.

    Wood chip or shredded bark mulch — 4–5 bags Buy at: WSN, Swanson, or Home Depot
  • 04
    Call West Seattle Nursery (206) 935-9276 to confirm Stewartia and Carex testacea are in stock and hold if possible. Review Swanson pricing as a comparison: Stewartia 6ft B&B = $124.99, Carex 1-gal = $19.99 each.
Do not purchase plants yet. Wait until nematode treatment (July) is complete before replanting ground cover. The Stewartia can go in now if drainage is confirmed acceptable.
Late May – Early June 2026
Step 5 — Plant
Replant Japanese Stewartia only
+
  • 01
    Purchase 1 Japanese Stewartia (6ft, balled and burlap) from Swanson Nursery. Keep receipt — Swanson guarantees all plants for their lifetime.

    Japanese Stewartia 6ft B&B — $124.99 Planting service (optional) — $125.00 Swanson Nursery
  • 02
    Plant in the amended hole at the same location, confirming drainage is acceptable first. Water deeply at planting and once weekly thereafter during dry periods.
  • 03
    Apply 2–3 inches of wood chip mulch around the base to the drip line, keeping it 6 inches away from the trunk. This moderates soil temperature and retains moisture, reducing transplant stress.
  • 04
    Do NOT plant ground cover yet. Hold off on Carex/fescue replacement until after the July nematode treatment eliminates grubs from the root zone.
Note on beetle risk: Japanese Stewartia is not a host species for bronze birch borer. The prior Stewartia death was likely site-stress related (drainage, root competition, grub damage), not beetle attack. Fixing those conditions is the priority. ↗ OSU Extension: Homeowner Guide to Managing Bronze Birch Borer↗ Swanson Nursery in-person consultation, March 2026
June–July 2026
Step 6 — Treat
Critical window: beneficial nematodes for new egg hatch
+
  • 01
    Adult beetles are laying eggs now. This is the single most effective treatment window of the year. Call WSN to confirm live nematodes are in stock before heading in. They sell out in season.

    Beneficial Nematodes — Heterorhabditis bacteriophora (Hb) strain Buy at: West Seattle Nursery — refrigerated, ask staff
  • 02
    Buy fresh and refrigerate until same-day use. Do not freeze. Use within 2–3 days of purchase.
  • 03
    Water the entire treatment area deeply the day before application. Moist soil is essential — nematodes cannot travel through dry soil.
  • 04
    Mix nematodes in a bucket of cool (not hot) water per package instructions. Stir gently. Pour into a hose-end sprayer, watering can, or pump sprayer with filter screen removed.
  • 05
    Apply in the evening or on a cloudy day. UV light kills nematodes. Cover the entire easement area including around the Stewartia root zone.
  • 06
    Water the area again immediately after application to drive nematodes 2–3 inches into the soil.
  • 07
    Keep soil moist by watering twice per week for 3 weeks after application. If soil dries out, nematodes die. This is non-negotiable.
  • 08
    Repeat nematode application in late July. Two applications in this window are significantly more effective than one. Plan to coordinate with neighbors for a joint purchase.
Expected result: 60–70% reduction in grub population. This is the biological standard for nematode treatment on European chafer in Western Washington. ↗ In Harmony Sustainable Landscapes: Chafer Beetle Invades Washington↗ WSN: European Chafer Grubs (Mar 2025)
Late July – August 2026
Step 7 — Plant
Replant ground cover: Carex testacea & alternatives
+
  • 01
    After nematode treatment is complete (minimum 3 weeks post-application), the soil is safe to replant. Purchase ground cover from Swanson or West Seattle Nursery.

    Carex testacea — 1-gal $19.99 ea (Swanson) Ice Plant 4" container — $6.99–$8.99 (Swanson) Keep all receipts — Swanson lifetime guarantee
  • 02
    Ask WSN about Carex testacea resilience and whether it is suited to the ground-level beds (not just the raised bed where it's currently surviving). They know West Seattle soil conditions specifically.
  • 03
    Plant in the evening or on a cool overcast day to reduce transplant stress. Water immediately and keep consistently moist for the first 3–4 weeks.
  • 04
    Apply GrubGONE! again around new plantings as a curative pass for any new generation of grubs hatching in late summer.
Aug – Oct 2026
Step 8 — Treat
Fall curative: protect new plantings through winter
+
  • 01
    Apply a third GrubGONE! treatment in August/September targeting the new generation of grubs before they grow large enough to cause serious root damage. Same application method as Phase 2.
  • 02
    Scout the lawn again in September. Dig several 12"×12" squares across the easement area and count grubs. If numbers are still high (10+ per sq ft), consider a professional spray service with soil penetrant — ask WSN for referrals.
  • 03
    Top up mulch before winter (3–4 bags). By October, the first round will have broken down slightly. Refresh to 2–3 inches across the full easement area, especially around the Stewartia root zone, to insulate roots through winter. Again, keep 6 inches clear of the trunk.

    Shredded bark mulch — 3–4 bags Buy at: WSN, Swanson, or Home Depot
  • 04
    Monitor Stewartia through fall. Ensure it's getting deep weekly watering during any dry stretches. Apply additional mulch before winter if needed (4" maximum, away from trunk).
Wildlife note: If you see crows or raccoons actively digging up the lawn in fall, grubs are still present in meaningful numbers. This is a signal to retreat, not a sign of treatment failure — it means the population is reduced but not eliminated. Continue the cycle next spring. ↗ Seattle Times: European Chafer & West Seattle↗ Aerating Thatching Co: European Chafer in Seattle
Spring 2027
Step 9 — Maintain
Year two: repeat treatment cycle & assess plantings
+
  • 01
    April: Scout for grubs again. Repeat GrubGONE! spring application as preventive maintenance.
  • 02
    June–July: Repeat nematode treatment. This is an annual cycle for as long as European chafer is present in the neighborhood (indefinitely, per WSN).
  • 03
    Assess Stewartia and Carex. If the Stewartia shows any signs of stress, contact Swanson and invoke the lifetime guarantee. Keep your original receipt.
  • 04
    Consider long-term ground cover strategy with WSN. If Carex continues to underperform in ground-level beds, ask about replacing sections with a pollinator garden or native ground covers that are naturally grub-resistant (WSN's own recommendation).
